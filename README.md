# **1.0 Introduction**
Saccharomyces cerevisiae flor strains form a structured floating biofilm, termed a velum, at the air–liquid interface during the biological aging of sherry-style wines. This aerobic lifestyle enables oxidative metabolism under conditions of low nutrient availability, elevated ethanol concentration, and oxidative stress, driving the accumulation of acetaldehyde, acetals, and other flavor-active compounds characteristic of fino and manzanilla wines (Moreno-García et al., 2017). Velum development progresses through distinct morphological stages—early, thin, and mature—and prior transcriptomic and proteomic work has demonstrated stage-specific remodeling of gene expression, including strong induction of the cell-adhesion gene FLO11 and broad activation of stress-response and respiratory programs during maturation (Mardanov et al., 2020; Moreno-García et al., 2017).

The present study analyzes bulk RNA-seq data from BioProject PRJNA592304, spanning three velum stages with 3 biological replicates per stage, with three objectives: (i) robustly quantify transcript abundance, (ii) identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs) across stage-to-stage contrasts, and (iii) perform functional enrichment analysis to connect transcriptomic changes with the biological processes underlying velum maturation.

Transcript quantification will be performed using Salmon in selective-alignment, decoy-aware mode against the S. cerevisiae Ensembl R64-1-1 reference. Selective alignment improves mapping sensitivity over lightweight k-mer hashing alone, while genomic decoys reduce spurious assignment of reads from unannotated loci—both are meaningful considerations in the compact yeast genome (Patro et al., 2017). Raw read quality will be assessed with FastQC and aggregated using MultiQC to detect per-sample outliers prior to downstream analysis (Andrews, 2010; Ewels et al., 2016). Differential expression will be tested with DESeq2, which employs negative-binomial generalized linear models with median-of-ratios normalization and empirical Bayes shrinkage—a framework well-suited to small replicate designs (Love et al., 2014). Finally, over-representation analysis and/or gene-set enrichment analysis will be applied to ranked DEG lists to contextualize findings within established pathways of adhesion, respiration, and stress response relevant to velum biology.

Each tool carries trade-offs. Salmon does not produce genome-aligned BAM files, precluding visualization of novel splice junctions—a minor limitation in the well-annotated yeast genome but relevant to consider. DESeq2's shrinkage estimators stabilize variance at low counts but assume independence across genes. Together, these methods provide a robust, multi-framework approach to characterizing the transcriptional architecture of flor yeast velum development.

## **2.2 Data & Metadata**

### **2.2.1 Data Download**
**Raw Sequencing Data Acquisition**
Raw RNA-seq reads were obtained from the NCBI Sequence Read Archive under BioProject accession PRJNA592304 (Mardanov et al., 2020). The dataset comprises nine single-end Illumina samples derived from Saccharomyces cerevisiae flor yeast at three stages of velum development: Early biofilm (IL20, IL21, IL22; SRR10551665–SRR10551663), Thin biofilm (IL23, IL24, IL25; SRR10551662–SRR10551660), and Mature biofilm (IL29, IL30, IL31; SRR10551659–SRR10551657), with three biological replicates per stage. Reads were downloaded using fasterq-dump (v3.2.1) from SRA-tools via a manifest-driven approach, with SRR accessions programmatically mapped to biological sample identifiers; full implementation details are provided in scripts/download.sh. Transcript abundance was quantified against the S. cerevisiae Ensembl R64-1-1 reference transcriptome (Saccharomyces_cerevisiae.R64-1-1.110) using Salmon in selective-alignment, decoy-aware mode.

**Reference Genome and Annotation Retrieval**
The Saccharomyces cerevisiae R64-1-1 reference assembly and its corresponding functional annotations were obtained from the Ensembl database (Release 110). Specifically, the cDNA sequences, the top-level genomic DNA assembly, and the Gene Transfer Format (GTF) annotation files were downloaded using the wget utility. These genomic resources provided the necessary framework for subsequent transcript quantification and analysis. To ensure high-performance mapping, the reference transcriptome was indexed using Salmon (v1.10.3). 


### **2.2.2 Metadata File**
A sample metadata file (samplesheet.csv) was constructed manually to map each sample identifier to its corresponding SRA run accession and velum stage, containing three columns: sample (e.g., IL20), srr (e.g., SRR10551665), and stage (Early, Thin, or Mature). The stage variable was encoded as an ordered factor with levels Early < Thin < Mature to reflect the biological progression of velum development, and was used as the grouping variable in all downstream DESeq2 models. No additional physicochemical metadata (e.g., ethanol content, aldehyde concentrations) reported in the original publication were incorporated into the statistical models, as the analysis was restricted to transcriptomic stage comparisons using the RNA-seq data alone.

To synthesize the individual sample reports into a single, interpretable diagnostic overview, MultiQC was employed to aggregate the results into a unified HTML report. Adapter trimming was not performed. Inspection of the FastQC and MultiQC reports confirmed that raw read quality was high across all nine samples, with no evidence of adapter contamination or systematic quality degradation. As Salmon's selective-alignment mode is robust to residual low-quality bases at read termini, trimming was deemed unnecessary, consistent with Salmon's documented robustness to minor quality variation in selective-alignment mode (Patro et al., 2017)

## **2.4 Reference & Indexing**
To ensure high-performance mapping and reduce spurious alignments from genomic contamination, a "gentrome" index was constructed. This involved concatenating the transcriptome and the whole-genome sequences, with the latter serving as a decoy to prevent reads originating from unannotated genomic loci from being spuriously assigned to annotated transcripts (Srivastava et al., 2020). Genome targets were identified and extracted into a decoy list to guide the mapping process. The final selective-alignment index was generated using Salmon (v1.10.3) with the default k-mer length (k=31), sufficient for the compact and well-annotated yeast transcriptome, providing the computational framework for subsequent transcript quantification. For the exact source URLs and retrieval parameters used in this step, please refer to the reference download section within the scripts directory.
```

# build the "gentrome" (transcriptome + genome concatenated; genome last)

# make the index

```
## **2.5 Quantification**
Transcript abundance for the nine biofilm samples was quantified using Salmon (v1.10.3) in its mapping-based quantification mode. Single-end reads were processed by aligning against the previously constructed S. cerevisiae R64-1-1 selective-alignment ("gentrome") index. To account for library-specific artifacts, the Salmon automatic library type detection (-l A) was employed. Quantification fidelity was enhanced through --gcBias, which corrects for fragment-level GC content biases introduced during library preparation, and --seqBias, which corrects for sequence-specific biases at read starts arising from random hexamer priming. The --validateMappings flag was included to enable stricter alignment scoring, reducing spurious mappings as recommended for selective-alignment mode (Srivastava et al., 2020). Calculations were executed across 16 parallel threads per sample to optimize computational throughput. The resulting quantification output files (quant.sf) provided both raw counts and normalized abundance estimates (Transcripts Per Million; TPM) for each transcript, which served as the foundation for subsequent differential gene expression analysis across the early, thin, and mature biofilm developmental stages. Full implementation details are provided in scripts folder.

## **2.6 Differential Expression Analysis**
Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 (v1.50.2; Love et al., 2014), with transcript-level abundance estimates imported via tximport using the countsFromAbundance = "no" setting to retain raw inferred counts, as DESeq2 requires unadjusted count data and performs its own internal normalisation (Love et al., 2014). A negative-binomial generalised linear model was fitted with velum stage (Early, Thin, Mature) as the sole explanatory variable. Genes with fewer than 10 counts in fewer than three samples were removed prior to modelling. Size factors were estimated using DESeq2's median-of-ratios method, and dispersion was estimated with empirical Bayes shrinkage. Log2 fold changes were further shrunk using the adaptive shrinkage estimator (ashr; Stephens, 2017) to reduce noise for low-count genes. A Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) threshold of 0.05 and an absolute log2 fold-change threshold of 1 were applied to define differentially expressed genes (DEGs).

### **2.6.1 PCA & Sample Distances**
To assess overall transcriptomic structure and sample reproducibility, count data were transformed using the variance-stabilising transformation (VST) implemented in DESeq2 with blind = FALSE so that the experimental design informs dispersion estimation rather than treating all samples as homogeneous — appropriate when samples differ substantially by condition (Love et al., 2014). Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on the VST-normalised matrix and visualised using ggplot2, with sample labels added via ggrepel to facilitate identification of potential outliers. Sample-to-sample Euclidean distances were computed on the transposed VST matrix and displayed as a hierarchically clustered heatmap using pheatmap, with samples annotated by velum stage. These diagnostics were used to confirm that biological replicates clustered by stage prior to statistical testing.

### **2.6.2 Pairwise Contrasts**
Three pairwise contrasts were extracted from the fitted DESeq2 model to characterise transcriptional changes at successive stages of velum development: Thin vs Early, Mature vs Early, and Mature vs Thin. For each contrast, results were obtained using the results() function with an independent filtering alpha of 0.05. Volcano plots were generated for all three contrasts, displaying shrunk log2 fold changes against -log10(adjusted p-value), with the top ten up- and down-regulated genes labelled by gene identifier. A combined three-panel figure was produced using the patchwork package to facilitate direct visual comparison across contrasts. Full results tables were exported as CSV files for downstream interpretation.

### **2.6.3 Likelihood Ratio Test**
To identify genes exhibiting any significant variation in expression across the three velum stages, irrespective of the direction or stage of change, a likelihood ratio test (LRT) was performed by comparing the full model (~stage) against a reduced intercept-only model (~1). This approach detects genes with a stage-dependent expression profile without being restricted to a single pairwise comparison, and is particularly suited to ordered developmental time-series such as the Early-Thin-Mature progression. Genes were considered significant at FDR < 0.05, and the resulting gene set was used to generate a clustered heatmap of the top 50 stage-varying genes to visualise temporal expression patterns.

## **2.7 Functional Annotation & Enrichment**
Functional enrichment analyses were performed to contextualise DEGs within known biological processes relevant to velum formation, including cell adhesion, oxidative metabolism, and stress response. Gene identifiers used throughout were Saccharomyces Genome Database (SGD) open reading frame (ORF) designations, compatible with the org.Sc.sgd.db annotation package (Bioconductor).

### **2.7.1 Over-Representation Analysis (ORA)**
Over-representation analysis (ORA) was conducted using the enrichGO() function from clusterProfiler (Wu et al., 2021), testing for enrichment of Gene Ontology Biological Process (GO:BP) terms among DEGs from all three pairwise contrasts (Thin vs Early, Mature vs Early, and Mature vs Thin). The background gene universe was defined as all genes passing the pre-filtering step. P-values were adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg method, and terms with adjusted p < 0.05 and q < 0.2 were considered significantly enriched. Results were visualised as dot plots showing the top 15 enriched terms per contrast, combined into a single three-panel figure using patchwork to enable direct cross-contrast comparison, with dot size proportional to gene-set size and colour indicating adjusted p-value.

### **2.7.2 Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA)**
Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was performed using the gseGO() function from clusterProfiler to capture coordinated shifts in GO:BP terms without imposing a binary significance threshold (Wu et al., 2021). Genes were pre-ranked by the DESeq2 Wald statistic from the Mature vs Early contrast, which integrates both fold-change magnitude and statistical confidence (Love et al., 2014). The analysis was run with 1,000 permutations and a minimum gene-set size of 10. Normalised enrichment scores (NES) and FDR-adjusted p-values were reported, and the top positively and negatively enriched pathways were visualised using enrichplot. GSEA complements ORA by detecting pathways enriched among moderately but consistently regulated genes that may fall below a strict fold-change cut-off.

### 2.7.3 Targeted Gene Expression Trajectories
To connect transcriptome-wide enrichment patterns to specific, biologically characterised genes, VST-normalised expression trajectories were plotted for five genes selected a priori on the basis of established functional roles in velum physiology and sherry wine chemistry. FLO11 (YHR211W) was selected as the master regulator of cell adhesion and velum formation, whose deletion abolishes biofilm entirely (Bruckner & Mosch, 2012). CYC1 (YJR048W) encodes iso-1 cytochrome c, the principal electron carrier linking Complexes III and IV; its transcription is activated up to 200-fold by oxygen via the HAP1 and HAP2/3/4/5 complexes (Guarente & Mason, 1983), making it a mechanistic marker for the aerobic respiration GO enrichment observed in the Mature contrasts. QCR2 (YPR191W) encodes a core subunit of mitochondrial Complex III; its deletion impairs growth on non-fermentable carbon sources, directly relevant to the ethanol-as-carbon-source environment of the velum, and connects to the electron transport chain terms enriched in the Mature vs Thin GO panel. ALD4 (YOR374W) and ALD6 (YPL061W) encode the mitochondrial and cytosolic aldehyde dehydrogenase isoforms respectively; their co-regulation in flor yeasts under acetaldehyde and ethanol stress has been demonstrated experimentally (Lasanta et al., 2003), and their induction provides a direct transcriptomic link to acetaldehyde accumulation that defines biologically-aged sherry wines (Navarro-Avino et al., 1999). VST counts were extracted from the DESeq2 vsd object for the five target ORFs, reshaped into long format using pivot_longer(), and stage means and standard errors were computed per gene using dplyr (Wickham et al., 2019). Trajectories were visualised using ggplot2, with stage mean connected by lines, ±1 SE displayed as a shaded ribbon via geom_ribbon(), and individual replicate values overlaid as jittered points via geom_jitter() (Wickham, 2016). Panels were faceted by gene using facet_wrap() with free y-axis scales to accommodate differences in absolute expression levels between genes. Figures were exported at 300 dpi using ggsave().

# **3.0 Results**

## 3.1 Transcriptome-Wide Structure and Sample Quality
To assess global transcriptomic structure and confirm replicate reproducibility prior to differential expression testing, variance-stabilised (VST) count data were subjected to principal component analysis (PCA) and pairwise Euclidean distance profiling. PCA revealed clear separation of all three velum stages along the first two principal components, which together explained 95% of total variance (PC1: 75%, PC2: 20%; Figure 1). Early biofilm replicates (IL20–IL22) clustered tightly in the lower-left quadrant, Thin biofilm samples (IL23–IL25) in the upper-left to upper-centre region, and Mature biofilm samples (IL29–IL31) in the lower-right quadrant. The clean stage-wise separation, combined with tight within-group clustering , although one Thin replicate (IL24) showed slightly greater separation along PC1, remaining within the Thin cluster but at greater distance from IL23 and IL25, indicated high biological reproducibility and confirmed that stage is the dominant source of transcriptional variance in this dataset. Sample-to-sample Euclidean distance analysis corroborated these findings (Figure 2). Hierarchical clustering of the distance matrix grouped samples by stage, with each group forming a clearly demarcated block of low pairwise distances along the diagonal. Between-stage distances were consistently larger than within-stage distances, and no outlier samples were identified. Together, Figures 1 and 2 provide strong evidence that the dataset is of sufficient quality for downstream differential expression analysis.

## **3.2 Differential Expression Across Velum Development Stages**
Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 with ashr log2 fold-change (LFC) shrinkage, applying thresholds of |LFC| > 1 and FDR < 0.05. Across all three pairwise contrasts, large numbers of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified, consistent with the extensive transcriptional remodelling reported for flor yeast velum formation (Mardanov et al., 2020). The Thin vs Early contrast yielded 840 DEGs (410 down-regulated, 430 up-regulated), reflecting the initial transcriptional shift upon biofilm initiation. The Mature vs Early contrast produced the largest number of DEGs (2,116 total; 961 down, 1,155 up), indicating a cumulative and progressive transcriptional divergence over velum development. The Mature vs Thin contrast identified 1,526 DEGs (649 down, 877 up), demonstrating that substantial transcriptional change continues between the thin and mature biofilm stages.

Volcano plots for all three contrasts are shown in Figure 3. Notably, several genes appeared as top-ranked DEGs across multiple contrasts. YGR088W, YGR087C, and YHR094C were among the most significantly down-regulated genes in both the Thin vs Early and Mature vs Early comparisons, suggesting suppression of these loci early in velum development that is maintained through maturation. Conversely, YNR073C, YNR071C, and YNR072W were consistently up-regulated in the Mature vs Early and Mature vs Thin contrasts, pointing to genes with specific roles in mature biofilm physiology. The Mature vs Early contrast additionally showed strong induction of YJR152W and YGL055W, genes associated with oxidative and respiratory metabolism, consistent with the known shift toward aerobic respiration during velum maturation.

## **3.3 Functional Enrichment Analysis**
GO biological process ORA across all three contrasts revealed both shared and contrast-specific enrichment patterns (Figure 6) (clusterProfiler v4.18.4; Wu et al., 2021). Transmembrane transport was the top-ranked term in all three comparisons, and in the Thin vs Early contrast it showed the highest GeneRatio (~0.15) with the strongest significance (p.adjust ~1×10⁻¹⁵), indicating that remodelling of membrane transport capacity is an early and dominant feature of velum initiation. The Thin vs Early panel was further enriched for organic acid, oxoacid, and carboxylic acid metabolic processes, and carbohydrate metabolic process — terms reflecting broad carbon metabolic rewiring at biofilm onset that are not as prominent in the later contrasts. The Mature vs Early contrast was dominated by respiratory pathway terms including cellular respiration, aerobic respiration, and energy derivation by oxidation of organic compounds, alongside fermentative pathway terms (hexose catabolism, glycolytic processes, fermentation), consistent with the co-existence of oxidative and glycolytic metabolism in bulk velum tissue (Mardanov et al., 2020). The Mature vs Thin panel showed the most specific enrichment for mitochondrial and electron transport chain processes — including oxidative phosphorylation, respiratory electron transport chain, aerobic electron transport chain, and mitochondrial ATP synthesis coupled electron transport — indicating that the final respiratory maturation of velum cells, at the level of the electron transport chain itself, occurs primarily between the Thin and Mature stages. Amino acid transport and organic anion transport were also specifically enriched in the Mature vs Thin contrast, pointing to further specialisation of nutrient acquisition systems in the fully formed velum. Together these three panels demonstrate a clear sequential logic: early velum formation involves broad metabolic and transport reprogramming; intermediate stages shift toward respiratory energy metabolism; and final maturation is characterised by induction of the mitochondrial respiratory machinery, consistent with the transcriptional and proteomic evidence for progressive respiratory activation in flor yeast velum (Mardanov et al., 2020; Moreno-García et al., 2017).

# **4.0 Discussion**
## **4.1 Biological Interpretation**

The present analysis reveals a progressive and large-scale transcriptional remodelling across the three stages of flor yeast velum development, yielding 840, 2,116, and 1,526 DEGs in the Thin vs Early, Mature vs Early, and Mature vs Thin contrasts, respectively (Figure 3). The magnitude and directionality of these changes are broadly consistent with the transcriptomic profiling reported by Mardanov et al. (2020) for the same BioProject dataset, who identified 1,490 stage-associated DEGs. Differences in absolute counts likely reflect differences in filtering stringency and LFC thresholds rather than true biological disagreement. Taken together, these results reinforce the conclusion that velum maturation in S. cerevisiae is not a binary switch but a staged and cumulative transcriptional programme. Of these DEGs, only 160 were shared across all three contrasts (Figure 4), 
confirming that the majority of transcriptional changes are stage-transition-specific rather than globally sustained, with the largest overlap of 1,057 genes occurring exclusively between the Mature vs Early and Mature vs Thin contrasts and reflecting a mature-stage transcriptional signature absent from the earliest biofilm transition.

Among the most consistently down-regulated genes from the earliest stages of velum development were YGR088W, YGR087C, and YHR094C (Figure 3). These loci are suppressed significantly in both the Thin vs Early and Mature vs Early contrasts, indicating that their repression is an early and sustained feature of biofilm initiation. The yeast genome contains a large number of ORFs whose functions remain incompletely characterised (Giaever et al., 2002); however, the consistent suppression of this cluster suggests these genes are associated with the planktonic, fermentative physiological state that is progressively dismantled as velum architecture is established. Their down-regulation may also reflect a broad reduction in glycolytic gene expression consistent with the diauxic shift and glucose exhaustion that precedes velum formation (Mardanov et al., 2020).

In contrast, YNR071C, YNR072W, and YNR073C were among the most strongly and specifically up-regulated genes in the Mature stage, appearing prominently across all three contrasts in the volcano plots (Figure 3). This locus neighbours genes involved in nitrogen and carbon metabolite sensing. Their induction specifically during the maturation phase—rather than at biofilm initiation—suggests a role in the metabolic adaptation of cells to the nutrient-restricted and ethanol-rich environment of the mature velum, consistent with the broad transcriptional reorientation toward non-fermentable carbon utilisation described in flor yeasts (Zara et al., 2009).

The GO biological process enrichment analysis (Figure 6) corroborates this metabolic reorientation at the pathway level. The most strongly enriched terms in the Mature vs Early contrast were transmembrane transport, cellular respiration, aerobic respiration, and generation of precursor metabolites and energy, all with adjusted p-values below 1×10⁻⁸. This pattern is mechanistically consistent with known transcriptional responses to ethanol-based carbon metabolism in S. cerevisiae, which requires derepression of TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, and high-affinity nutrient transport genes (Forsburg & Guarente, 1989). The co-enrichment of fermentative pathway terms (hexose catabolism, glycolytic processes, fermentation) likely reflects the mixed metabolic state captured in bulk velum tissue, as cells at different depths within the biofilm are exposed to markedly different oxygen concentrations (Mardanov et al., 2020). Notably, the Thin vs Early contrast showed a distinct enrichment pattern (Figure 6, left panel), with terms related to earlier biosynthetic and cell growth processes, while the Mature vs Thin contrast (right panel) was dominated by transport and energy terms, suggesting the respiratory transition is completed primarily between the Thin and Mature stages.

Although FLO11 did not appear among the top 50 DEGs shown in Figure 5, its biological importance in velum formation is well established. FLO11 encodes a mucin-like cell wall glycoprotein that mediates homotypic cell-cell adhesion and confers the buoyancy required for velum formation at the air-liquid interface (Brückner & Mösch, 2012). Deletion of FLO11 abolishes biofilm formation entirely, and its expression in flor strains is driven by an unusually large and complex promoter integrating inputs from the cAMP-PKA, MAPK, and TOR signalling pathways (Torbensen et al., 2012; Brückner & Mösch, 2012). Mardanov et al. (2020) reported that FLO11 accounts for >99% of flocculin transcripts in mature velum; its absence from the top 50 by adjusted p-value in our analysis reflects the ranking method rather than biological insignificance, as genes with very low dispersion and moderate but consistent fold changes may not rank highest by FDR. The proteomic data of Moreno-García et al. (2017) independently confirmed elevated Flo11p protein abundance in the biofilm state, providing multi-omics corroboration of FLO11 importance in the biofilm state; however, the trajectory analysis (Figure 7) showed declining FLO11 transcript levels across stages, suggesting post-transcriptional regulation or that adhesion requirements are met at lower transcript levels once biofilm architecture is established. 

The progressive induction of ALD4 and ALD6, encoding mitochondrial and cytosolic aldehyde dehydrogenase isoforms respectively, is among the most biologically and enologically significant findings of this analysis. These enzymes catalyse the oxidation of acetaldehyde to acetate, channelling the products of ethanol catabolism into the TCA cycle (Navarro-Avino et al., 1999). Their co-induction in mature velum has been experimentally demonstrated in flor yeasts under acetaldehyde and ethanol stress (Lasanta et al., 2003), and the accumulation of acetaldehyde in biologically-aged sherry wines is a direct consequence of this enzymatic activity. The transcriptional data presented here therefore provide a molecular-level explanation for a centuries-old winemaking phenomenon.


## **4.2 Implications**

The progressive accumulation of DEGs across the developmental axis—with each transition adding hundreds of newly regulated genes—implies that velum maturation requires sustained transcriptional input rather than a single committed developmental switch. The predominantly stage-transition-specific nature of these changes (only 160 genes shared across all three contrasts; Figure 4) further implies that velum competence is acquired incrementally, and that different molecular targets may be relevant at different stages of commercial velum establishment.This has direct relevance to the biological ageing of sherry-style wines: the accumulation of acetaldehyde and acetals, the principal chemical signatures of biological ageing, is driven by oxidative ethanol metabolism that is only fully active in the mature velum (Mardanov et al., 2020). Understanding the transcriptional determinants of this transition could inform selection of flor strains with superior oxidative capacity or provide molecular targets for optimising velum establishment in commercial wine production.

The strong enrichment of transmembrane transport processes across all three contrasts (Figure 6) also has applied significance. Flor yeast cells in mature velum must acquire nutrients from a carbon-limited medium while exporting ethanol oxidation products. Induction of high-affinity transport systems is an anticipated adaptive response, and the specific transporters up-regulated here could serve as biomarkers of velum maturity or as engineering targets for strains with improved survival under harsh wine conditions.
